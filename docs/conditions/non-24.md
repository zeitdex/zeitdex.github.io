# Non-24-Hour Sleep-Wake Disorder

**Non-24** is a circadian rhythm sleep-wake disorder in which a person's internal clock is not entrained to the 24-hour day. The sleep-wake cycle runs longer (or, rarely, shorter) than 24 hours, so sleep timing **drifts a little each day — usually later** — cycling all the way around the clock over weeks.

It is common in people who are **totally blind** (with no light perception, the strongest zeitgeber cannot entrain the clock), and it also occurs in sighted people.

!!! warning "Not medical advice"
    Diagnosis and treatment of Non-24 belong with a qualified clinician. This page is a community overview, not a substitute for medical care.

## Common features

- Sleep onset and wake time that shift progressively later (or earlier) over days to weeks.
- Alternating "good" and "bad" stretches as the rhythm cycles in and out of phase with the desired schedule — periods of daytime sleepiness and night-time wakefulness in between.
- Difficulty holding a fixed social, school, or work schedule.

## Long-period and short-period Non-24 (delayed vs advanced)

Non-24 comes in two directions, set by whether the clock's natural period is longer or shorter than 24 hours:

- **Delayed (long-period) Non-24** is by far the usual form. The intrinsic period is **longer than 24 hours**, so each cycle the body runs a little long and sleep begins later than the day before. This direction dominates partly because the average human clock already runs a touch over 24 hours, and light and modern habits tend to push it later still.
- **Advanced (short-period) Non-24** is the rare mirror image. The intrinsic period is **shorter than 24 hours**, so sleep creeps *earlier* each day. It is documented, but much less common.

The further the period sits from 24 hours, the **faster the drift**: a ~25-hour clock moves roughly an hour later each day; a ~23-hour clock, roughly an hour earlier.

At the extreme, a very short or very erratic period starts to blur toward [irregular sleep-wake rhythm disorder](other/iswrd.md) — the difference being that Non-24 keeps a **strong, predictable march**, whereas ISWRD has no coherent pattern to follow.

!!! note "A critical note — short cycles need hard proof"
    Unusually short free-running periods are rarely documented and easy to misjudge from a glance at a messy schedule. Precisely because they're so uncommon, a claim of a very short cycle really wants objective backing — extended [actigraphy](diagnosis.md), and melatonin-timing (DLMO) testing where it's available — before being taken at face value.

## Seeing the drift

Because the hallmark is *drift*, a long, continuous record of sleep timing is the clearest way to see the pattern. [The Zeitlog tracker](https://zeitlog.github.io/) charts sleep over time, so a free-running rhythm shows up as a diagonal band marching across the chart.
